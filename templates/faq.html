⚡ Features

What is FCE Trainer?

A free web app for practicing the Cambridge B2 First (FCE) exam. It covers all 7 Use of English & Reading parts and the Writing section, with AI-generated tasks and instant feedback.

What exam parts are included?

PartTypeQuestions
1Multiple-choice cloze8 gaps
2Open cloze8 gaps
3Word formation8 gaps
4Key word transformations6 items
5Reading comprehension6 questions
6Gapped text6 gaps
7Multiple matching10 questions

Plus Writing Part 1 (compulsory essay) with AI evaluation, and Get Phrases for collocations with "get".

How are tasks generated?

Tasks are created by AI (OpenAI or Google Gemini) and stored in a database. You can generate new tasks at B2 or B2+ difficulty level. The app avoids showing you the same task twice within the last 100 shown tasks.

How do answer explanations work?

After you check your answers, AI analyses each gap and provides an explanation: why the correct answer fits, and what makes your answer wrong (if it was). For Part 3, you also get the full word family (e.g. happy → happily, unhappy, happiness).

Do I need an account?

You can practice without an account, but signing in (Google or email) saves your statistics, tracks your progress across devices, and enables spaced repetition reviews.

{# ── Spaced Repetition ── #}

🧠 Spaced Repetition

What is spaced repetition?

Spaced repetition is a learning technique based on the "forgetting curve" — the scientific finding that we forget information at a predictable rate. By reviewing material just as you're about to forget it, you strengthen the memory with minimal effort.

How does the SM-2 algorithm work?

FCE Trainer uses the SM-2 algorithm (the same algorithm that powers Anki). Here's how it works:

1
You practice a task and get some answers wrong

The system records this task and schedules your first review for tomorrow.

2
You review and get it right

The interval grows: next review in 3 days.

3
You get it right again

The interval grows more: next review in ~8 days, then ~20 days, then ~50 days…

✗
You get it wrong again?

The card resets — you'll see it again tomorrow. The cycle restarts.

How does quality scoring work?

Your score on each task is mapped to a quality grade (0–5):

100% Perfect recall
80%+ Correct with hesitation
60%+ Correct with difficulty
40%+ Incorrect — card resets
20%+ Incorrect — card resets
<20% Blackout — card resets

Scoring 60% or above advances the card to a longer interval. Below 60% resets it back to the start.

How do I know when a task is a review?

When you click "Next task" and there's a due review, the system automatically loads that task instead of a new one. You'll see a purple "🔁 Spaced Repetition Review" banner at the top of the task. The part tabs also show small badges with the number of due reviews.

Does it work for Part 4 (Key word transformations)?

Yes! Part 4 is special because each practice set has 6 individual sentences. The system tracks each sentence separately, so only the specific sentences you got wrong come back for review — not the entire set.

What if I get a perfect score on the first try?

No review card is created. The system assumes you already know this material well. Review cards are only created when you make mistakes.

📈 How It Helps Your Study

Why is spaced repetition effective?

Research shows that spaced practice is 2-3x more effective than massed practice (cramming). Here's why:

  • Fights the forgetting curve — You review at the optimal moment, just before you'd forget. This strengthens long-term memory.
  • Focuses on weaknesses — You spend time on the grammar and vocabulary you struggle with, not what you already know.
  • Saves time — Easy items space out to weeks or months. Difficult items stay frequent. You study less but remember more.
  • Builds confidence — Seeing your intervals grow proves you're actually learning, not just recognizing answers temporarily.

How does this compare to re-reading textbooks?

Re-reading gives a false sense of familiarity. It feels easy because you recognize the material, but recognition ≠ recall. Active retrieval (answering questions, filling gaps) forces your brain to actually reconstruct the knowledge — which is what the exam demands.

How should I use this for exam preparation?

  1. Practice daily — Even 15–20 minutes a day is more effective than a 3-hour cram session once a week.
  2. Do all 7 parts — Don't just focus on what's easy. The exam tests everything.
  3. Read the explanations — Don't just check right/wrong. Read why the correct answer works.
  4. Let reviews come to you — When a review task appears, take it seriously. That's the system targeting your weak spots.
  5. Track your stats — Watch your accuracy improve over time. Aim for 70%+ across all parts.

📝 Exam Parts Guide

Part 1 — Multiple-choice cloze

A text with 8 gaps. For each gap you choose from A, B, C, or D. Tests vocabulary: collocations, phrasal verbs, fixed expressions, and meaning in context. Tip: Read the whole sentence, not just the gap.

Part 2 — Open cloze

A text with 8 gaps. You write exactly one word in each gap. Tests grammar: articles, prepositions, pronouns, linking words, auxiliary verbs. Tip: The answer is almost always a "small" grammar word, not a content word.

Part 3 — Word formation

A text with 8 gaps. You're given a "stem" word and must form the correct word (noun, adjective, adverb, verb, negative prefix, etc.). Tests word building. Tip: Read around the gap to determine the word class needed (adjective? noun? negative?).

Part 4 — Key word transformations

6 pairs of sentences. You rewrite the second sentence using a given keyword (3–5 words). Tests grammar structures: passives, conditionals, reported speech, wish, phrasal verbs. Tip: The keyword must appear exactly as given — don't change its form.

Part 5 — Reading comprehension

A long text with 6 multiple-choice questions. Tests detailed understanding: opinion, attitude, purpose, main idea, text organisation. Tip: For each question, find the relevant paragraph first, then compare options carefully.

Part 6 — Gapped text

A text with 6 sentences removed. You put them back in the right gaps (7 options, 1 is extra). Tests text coherence: reference words, logical flow, linking. Tip: Look for pronouns (he, this, such) and linking phrases that connect to surrounding text.

Part 7 — Multiple matching

4–6 short texts and 10 statements. You match each statement to the correct text. Tests scanning and paraphrasing. Tip: The statements use different words from the text — look for paraphrases, not exact matches.

{# ── Tips ── #}

💡 Study Tips

How many tasks should I do per day?

Quality over quantity. 2–3 tasks per part with careful review of explanations is better than rushing through 20. Aim for 20–30 minutes of focused practice daily.

Which parts should I focus on?

Check your statistics page. Focus on parts where your accuracy is below 50%. Don't ignore reading (Parts 5–7) — they carry the most marks in the real exam.

How do I improve my vocabulary?

After each practice, read the explanations carefully. For Part 3, review the word families shown after checking. The spaced repetition system will bring back the tasks you struggled with, reinforcing the vocabulary over time.

What about Writing?

The Writing section generates Part 1 essay tasks. Write your answer (140–190 words), then submit for AI evaluation that scores your essay on content, communicative achievement, organisation, and language — the same criteria used by real Cambridge examiners.
